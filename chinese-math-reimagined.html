01 · 術 shù

Procedure over Proof 以術代理

Western mathematics rests on axioms and deductive chains going back to Euclid. Its foundational question: "Does this hold, from first principles?"

Chinese mathematics was built on shù — method, procedure, algorithm. Its foundational question: "Does this procedure always yield the correct result?"

Rigour exists in both traditions. But its source is different: not a fixed bedrock of axioms, but the operational invariance of a method across all valid inputs. Closer, in spirit, to what we now call a correctness proof for a program.

02 · 算籌 suàn chóu

A Grid-Based Notation 算籌符號

Without Arabic numerals or Latin letters, the native foundation would be counting-rod notation — a base-10 positional system using vertical and horizontal rod arrangements, placed on a physical grid.

Each cell position carries the semantic weight. The layout itself is the syntax. Unknowns are not labelled x or y — they are positions in an array.

03 · 方程術 fāngchéng shù

Array Algebra 方程術

Rather than equations written as strings of symbols, polynomial systems are represented as column arrays on a grid. Each column is a polynomial; the rows are coefficient degrees. This is not a metaphor — it is the literal notation of the 13th-century Tianyuan algebra.

What we call Gaussian elimination, they called fāngchéng shù — "the procedure of rectangular arrays." It preceded the European method by centuries.

04 · 出入相補 chūrù xiāng bǔ

Proof by Transformation 出入相補

Liu Hui's principle: "What goes out must equal what comes in." A geometric identity is proved by dissecting and rearranging — until the equality becomes visually self-evident.

This is not informal hand-waving. It is a formal rewrite system: each dissection step is constrained by rules of area-preservation, and the final arrangement constitutes the proof.

In a matured tradition, this evolves into a transformation logic — where the unit of reasoning is an area-preserving operation on a structure, rather than a logical implication between propositions.

05 · 穷竭 qióng jié

Constructive Calculus 算法微積

No limits, no infinitesimals. Instead: exhaustion as explicit algorithm. A derivative is not a static limit — it is the procedure "take the difference quotient, then narrow the interval." A real number is not a Dedekind cut (a completed infinite set) but a program that can generate any desired decimal digit on demand.

Zu Chongzhi computed π to 3.14159265 in the 5th century — not via abstract analysis, but by running the polygon-inscribing procedure further. In an unbroken tradition, this becomes the natural foundation for all of analysis.

06 · 算板語言

The Abacus as Programming Language 算板語言

If the grid-layout is the syntax, and rod-movements are operations, then the abacus board is literally a two-dimensional programming language. Each "computation" is a sequence of legal moves on the grid.

What we call a function is a 術 shù — a named procedure. What we call a variable is a column position. What we call a proof is a demonstration that a sequence of moves always produces the desired configuration.

This tradition, developed further, would have arrived at formal rewriting systems, symbolic computation, and something very close to combinatory logic — centuries before Schönfinkel.

07 · 得失

What Is Gained. What Is Lost. 得失

Gained

  • Computation and reasoning are unified from the start — the symbol is executable.
  • Linear algebra, polynomial systems, and combinatorics share a natural common notation (the array).
  • Constructive mathematics is the default — no set-theoretic foundations crisis in 1900.
  • Symbolic computation as a natural extension of traditional methods.
  • A geometric language of transformation and invariance, rather than of definitions and deduction.

Absent or Late

  • Set theory and the concept of actual infinity.
  • The axiomatic method and the idea of a mathematical "structure" defined by relations.
  • A sharp separation between geometry and number — they remain fused as "computable shapes."
  • Formal logic as an independent discipline.

"An entirely native Chinese mathematics would not be a stunted version of the Western tradition. It would be an alternative edifice — equally rigorous, but operating differently throughout. In it, operation and proof are inseparable, and the abacus board, not the straight-edge and compass, is the ultimate instrument of abstract thought."
