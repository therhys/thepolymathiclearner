01Lesson 1 — Iterative Functions: The Mathematics of “What Happens Next?”

We begin with one of the simplest but most powerful forms in mathematics:

\[ x_{t+1}=f(x_t) \]
x_{t+1}=f(x_t)

Read this slowly:

The next state is a function of the current state.

This is the seed of dynamical systems, complexity science, machine learning, state-space modelling, reinforcement learning, and even parts of FEP. It is also a much more natural way to enter mathematics than memorising formulas. Instead of asking “which formula do I apply?”, we ask:

  • What is the current state?
  • What rule transforms it?
  • What happens if we repeat the rule?
  • Does the system settle, explode, oscillate, or become unpredictable?

The symbol \(x_t\) means the state at time \(t\). The symbol \(x_{t+1}\) means the next state. The function \(f\) is the update rule.

A simple example is:

\[ x_{t+1}=0.8x_t+5 \]
x_{t+1}=0.8x_t+5

This says: keep 80% of the current state and add 5. If this represented a stock of something — money, energy, population, public trust, or attention — then each step both retains some past condition and adds a new input.

Now the interesting question is not just “what is the next value?” but:

What kind of behaviour does this rule generate if repeated?

That is the first complexity intuition. A mathematical rule is not merely an answer-getting device. It is a generator of behaviour.

When a state reproduces itself under the update rule, we call it a fixed point:

\[ x^*=f(x^*) \]
x^*=f(x^*)

Read:

\(x^*\) is a state that remains unchanged when the rule is applied.

In ordinary language, it is an equilibrium. But equilibrium does not mean “good.” It simply means self-reproducing. A dysfunctional organisation can be in equilibrium. An underused public space can be in equilibrium. A low-trust policy environment can be in equilibrium. Mathematics lets us ask whether a system stabilises, but design and ethics ask whether that stable state is desirable.

For your work, this distinction is important. In PTM or computational architecture, we may not only ask what variables exist. We ask what update rules make social, spatial, affective, or policy states reproduce themselves over time.

The conceptual takeaway:

A system is not only defined by what it is. It is defined by how it updates.
02Lesson 2 — Nonlinearity and the Logistic Map: When Simple Rules Become Complex

The next major step is to see how a very simple equation can generate surprisingly rich behaviour:

\[ x_{t+1}=rx_t(1-x_t) \]
x_{t+1}=rx_t(1-x_t)

This is the logistic map. It is famous in complexity science because it shows that complex behaviour does not always require complicated equations.

Decode the terms:

  • \(x_t\): current state, often interpreted as a population proportion between 0 and 1.
  • \(r\): growth parameter.
  • \(1-x_t\): limiting term, representing saturation or carrying capacity.

The equation contains a tension:

\[ x_t \]
x_t

supports growth, while:

\[ 1-x_t \]
1-x_t

limits growth as the system approaches capacity.

This matters because real systems often contain both expansion and constraint. A public space can attract more people, but too many people may reduce comfort. A brand can gain popularity, but overexposure may reduce desirability. A city can generate economic density, but congestion and cost can reduce livability. A policy can increase access, but create new pressures elsewhere.

The parameter \(r\) does not merely change the speed of the system. It changes the regime of behaviour. At different values of \(r\), the logistic map can settle to a fixed point, oscillate, double its period, or become chaotic.

This is a profound idea:

A parameter can be a regime switch, not merely a quantity.

In your PTM language, a parameter might be interest rates, housing elasticity, public trust, service reliability, demographic pressure, or institutional response time. In computational architecture, a parameter might be density, porosity, visibility, acoustic leakage, thermal comfort, or threshold strength.

The mathematical lesson is that nonlinear feedback can produce disproportionate outcomes. The design lesson is that small changes near critical thresholds may reorganise the whole system.

So rather than seeing equations as formulae to memorise, we begin to see them as:

compressed worlds of behaviour.
03Lesson 3 — Probability: From Certainty to Structured Uncertainty

Probability enters when we stop pretending that systems are perfectly deterministic. Instead of saying exactly what will happen, we say what could happen and with what likelihood.

\[ P(A) \]
P(A)

means the probability of event \(A\).

\[ P(A\mid B) \]
P(A\mid B)

means the probability of \(A\), given \(B\). The vertical bar \(\mid\) means “given.”

This is not just notation. It is a shift in thinking. A probability statement is always conditional on some state of knowledge or model. For example:

\[ P(\text{high dwell time}\mid \text{shade}) \]
P(\text{high dwell time}\mid \text{shade})

means:

Among spaces with shade, how likely is high dwell time?

But this does not yet mean shade causes dwell time. It only describes an observed or modelled conditional relation. Later, causal inference will teach us the difference between:

\[ P(Y\mid X) \]
P(Y\mid X)

and:

\[ P(Y\mid do(X)) \]
P(Y\mid do(X))

For now, probability gives us a language for uncertainty. In social, urban, architectural, organisational, and policy systems, uncertainty is not a nuisance to eliminate. It is part of the system.

A probability distribution assigns probabilities across possible outcomes:

\[ \sum_x p(x)=1 \]
\sum_x p(x)=1

This means the probabilities across all possible values sum to 1.

In your research world, probability becomes essential because the things you care about are not directly measurable in clean ways:

  • comfort
  • trust
  • social permission
  • institutional legitimacy
  • urban vitality
  • affective states
  • policy stress
  • organisational resilience

These are not simple objects. They are uncertain constructs inferred through signals.

The conceptual takeaway:

Probability is not merely about randomness. It is a disciplined language for uncertainty under assumptions.
04Lesson 4 — Bayesian Updating: Evidence, Priors, and Posterior Belief

Bayes’ theorem gives us one of the most important symbolic structures in all of modelling:

\[ P(H\mid D)=\frac{P(D\mid H)P(H)}{P(D)} \]
P(H\mid D)=\frac{P(D\mid H)P(H)}{P(D)}

Read:

The probability of a hypothesis after seeing data equals the likelihood of the data under that hypothesis, times prior belief, normalised by the probability of the data.

Decode the parts:

  • \(H\): hypothesis
  • \(D\): data
  • \(P(H)\): prior belief
  • \(P(D\mid H)\): likelihood
  • \(P(H\mid D)\): posterior belief
  • \(P(D)\): evidence or normalising constant

The crucial distinction is between:

\[ P(D\mid H) \]
P(D\mid H)

and:

\[ P(H\mid D) \]
P(H\mid D)

They are not the same. The first asks:

If the hypothesis were true, how likely would the data be?

The second asks:

After seeing the data, how likely is the hypothesis?

This distinction is everywhere. For example:

\[ P(\text{irritability}\mid \text{stress}) \]
P(\text{irritability}\mid \text{stress})

is not the same as:

\[ P(\text{stress}\mid \text{irritability}) \]
P(\text{stress}\mid \text{irritability})

In design research, this is powerful. Observing dwell time, gesture, facial expression, or movement does not directly reveal comfort, anxiety, or curiosity. It updates belief about those hidden states.

Bayesian thinking is also philosophically aligned with your intuition that mathematical claims are not absolute truth. A posterior is not truth itself. It is updated belief under a model, prior, and evidence.

The conceptual takeaway:

Evidence does not speak by itself. Evidence updates belief through a model.
05Lesson 5 — Markov Chains: Probabilistic Movement Through States

A Markov chain models probabilistic movement between states over time.

\[ P(X_{t+1}\mid X_t) \]
P(X_{t+1}\mid X_t)

Read:

The probability of the next state depends on the current state.

The Markov property says:

\[ P(X_{t+1}\mid X_t,X_{t-1},X_{t-2},\dots)=P(X_{t+1}\mid X_t) \]
P(X_{t+1}\mid X_t,X_{t-1},X_{t-2},\dots)=P(X_{t+1}\mid X_t)

This means that once we know the current state, the past adds no extra information for predicting the next state.

This does not mean history is irrelevant in reality. It means the model assumes the relevant history has been compressed into the present state. That is an important modelling assumption.

Transition probabilities can be written in a matrix:

\[ P= \begin{bmatrix} 0.8 & 0.2\\ 0.4 & 0.6 \end{bmatrix} \]
P=
\begin{bmatrix}
0.8 & 0.2\\
0.4 & 0.6
\end{bmatrix}

Rows are current states. Columns are next states. A probability distribution over states can be written as a vector:

\[ \mathbf{v}_t= \begin{bmatrix} 0.7 & 0.3 \end{bmatrix} \]
\mathbf{v}_t=
\begin{bmatrix}
0.7 & 0.3
\end{bmatrix}

The update is:

\[ \mathbf{v}_{t+1}=\mathbf{v}_tP \]
\mathbf{v}_{t+1}=\mathbf{v}_tP

This is beautiful because it shows linear algebra becoming a language of system evolution. The vector is the current probability landscape. The matrix is the transition structure. Multiplying them moves the system forward.

A stationary distribution satisfies:

\[ \boldsymbol{\pi}P=\boldsymbol{\pi} \]
\boldsymbol{\pi}P=\boldsymbol{\pi}

This means the distribution remains unchanged after applying the transition matrix.

For policy or architecture, this can model transitions between states such as:

  • comfortable / neutral / stressed
  • trusting / uncertain / distrustful
  • lingering / passing / avoiding
  • stable / vulnerable / crisis-prone

The conceptual takeaway:

A Markov chain is a probabilistic grammar of state-to-state movement.
06Lesson 6 — Hidden Markov Models: Observation Is Not Reality

A normal Markov chain assumes that we can directly observe the state. But in real systems, the state we care about is often hidden.

\[ X_t=\text{hidden state} \]
X_t=\text{hidden state}
\[ Y_t=\text{observation} \]
Y_t=\text{observation}

A Hidden Markov Model has two layers:

\[ P(X_{t+1}\mid X_t) \]
P(X_{t+1}\mid X_t)

which describes how hidden states evolve, and:

\[ P(Y_t\mid X_t) \]
P(Y_t\mid X_t)

which describes how hidden states generate observations.

The hidden state might be:

  • actual emotional state
  • public trust
  • spatial comfort
  • student understanding
  • market confidence
  • organisational morale

The observation might be:

  • facial expression
  • survey response
  • dwell time
  • test score
  • trading volume
  • employee feedback

The crucial lesson is:

The observation is not the hidden state. The observation is evidence generated from the hidden state.

For example, long dwell time may suggest comfort, but it may also mean waiting, confusion, social obligation, lack of exits, rain, charging a phone, or avoiding something else. So we should not write:

\[ \text{long dwell time}=\text{comfort} \]
\text{long dwell time}=\text{comfort}

Instead we ask:

\[ P(\text{comfort}\mid \text{long dwell time}) \]
P(\text{comfort}\mid \text{long dwell time})

This is where Bayes enters. The observation updates our belief about the hidden state.

Filtering over time asks:

\[ P(X_t\mid Y_1,\dots,Y_t) \]
P(X_t\mid Y_1,\dots,Y_t)

Read:

What is the current hidden state, given all observations so far?

This idea is foundational for FEP, state-space models, active inference, computational architecture, and PTM. It protects us from the common mistake of treating visible data as reality itself.

The conceptual takeaway:

Data is evidence about reality, not direct access to reality.
07Lesson 7 — Linear Algebra: Vectors, Matrices, and Transformations

Linear algebra is the mathematics of many quantities arranged together and transformed together.

A scalar is a single number:

\[ x=3 \]
x=3

A vector is a bundle of numbers:

\[ \mathbf{x}= \begin{bmatrix} 2\\5\\7 \end{bmatrix} \]
\mathbf{x}=
\begin{bmatrix}
2\\5\\7
\end{bmatrix}

A matrix is a rectangular structure of numbers:

\[ A= \begin{bmatrix} 1&2\\3&4 \end{bmatrix} \]
A=
\begin{bmatrix}
1&2\\3&4
\end{bmatrix}

In machine learning, a vector often represents a feature bundle. For example, a space might be represented as:

\[ \mathbf{x}= \begin{bmatrix} \text{openness}\\ \text{brightness}\\ \text{noise}\\ \text{thermal comfort} \end{bmatrix} \]
\mathbf{x}=
\begin{bmatrix}
\text{openness}\\
\text{brightness}\\
\text{noise}\\
\text{thermal comfort}
\end{bmatrix}

The dot product:

\[ \mathbf{w}\cdot \mathbf{x}=\sum_{i=1}^{n}w_ix_i \]
\mathbf{w}\cdot \mathbf{x}=\sum_{i=1}^{n}w_ix_i

means multiply corresponding components and add them up. This becomes a weighted score.

A simple predictive model is:

\[ \hat{y}=\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x}+b \]
\hat{y}=\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x}+b

Read:

Prediction equals weighted sum of features plus bias.

A matrix-vector product:

\[ A\mathbf{x} \]
A\mathbf{x}

means transforming vector \(\mathbf{x}\) using matrix \(A\). In neural networks, this becomes:

\[ \mathbf{h}=\sigma(W\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{b}) \]
\mathbf{h}=\sigma(W\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{b})

where \(W\) is a weight matrix, \(\mathbf{b}\) is bias, \(\sigma\) is a nonlinear activation, and \(\mathbf{h}\) is a hidden representation.

For computational architecture, this lets us imagine mapping spatial features into latent experiential dimensions:

\[ \mathbf{h}= \begin{bmatrix} \text{comfort}\\ \text{safety}\\ \text{sociability} \end{bmatrix} \]
\mathbf{h}=
\begin{bmatrix}
\text{comfort}\\
\text{safety}\\
\text{sociability}
\end{bmatrix}

The conceptual takeaway:

A vector describes what a thing is; a matrix describes how a thing is transformed.
08Lesson 8 — Calculus for Machine Learning: Change, Gradients, and Optimisation

Calculus gives machine learning its learning mechanism.

The derivative:

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} \]
\frac{dy}{dx}

means the rate of change of \(y\) with respect to \(x\). In machine learning, we care about how loss changes when parameters change:

\[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial w} \]
\frac{\partial L}{\partial w}

Read:

How sensitive is the loss \(L\) to the weight \(w\)?

A loss function measures error. For squared error:

\[ L=(y-\hat{y})^2 \]
L=(y-\hat{y})^2

where \(y\) is true value and \(\hat{y}\) is prediction.

Learning means adjusting parameters so loss becomes smaller. Gradient descent does this:

\[ \theta\leftarrow\theta-\eta\nabla L(\theta) \]
\theta\leftarrow\theta-\eta\nabla L(\theta)

Decode:

  • \(\theta\): model parameters
  • \(\eta\): learning rate
  • \(\nabla L(\theta)\): gradient of loss
  • \(\leftarrow\): update

The gradient points in the direction of increasing loss. So to reduce loss, we move against it.

For one parameter:

\[ w\leftarrow w-\eta\frac{\partial L}{\partial w} \]
w\leftarrow w-\eta\frac{\partial L}{\partial w}

Backpropagation computes gradients through a neural network. Gradient descent uses those gradients to update parameters.

This connects back to iterative functions:

\[ \theta_{t+1}=\theta_t-\eta\nabla L(\theta_t) \]
\theta_{t+1}=\theta_t-\eta\nabla L(\theta_t)

Machine learning training is itself a dynamical system: parameters move through a loss landscape.

The conceptual takeaway:

Machine learning learns by changing parameters in the direction that reduces error.
09Lesson 9 — Machine Learning Maths: From Linear Regression to Neural Networks

Supervised learning starts with data pairs:

\[ (x_1,y_1),(x_2,y_2),\dots,(x_N,y_N) \]
(x_1,y_1),(x_2,y_2),\dots,(x_N,y_N)

Each \(x_i\) is an input and \(y_i\) is the true output. A model predicts:

\[ \hat{y}_i=f_\theta(x_i) \]
\hat{y}_i=f_\theta(x_i)

The simplest model is linear regression:

\[ \hat{y}=\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x}+b \]
\hat{y}=\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x}+b

For one example, squared error loss is:

\[ \ell_i=(y_i-\hat{y}_i)^2 \]
\ell_i=(y_i-\hat{y}_i)^2

For the whole dataset:

\[ L(\mathbf{w},b)= \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} (y_i-(\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x}_i+b))^2 \]
L(\mathbf{w},b)=
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}
(y_i-(\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x}_i+b))^2

The training objective is:

\[ (\mathbf{w}^*,b^*)= \arg\min_{\mathbf{w},b}L(\mathbf{w},b) \]
(\mathbf{w}^*,b^*)=
\arg\min_{\mathbf{w},b}L(\mathbf{w},b)

For classification, logistic regression uses:

\[ \hat{p}=\sigma(\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x}+b) \]
\hat{p}=\sigma(\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x}+b)

where sigmoid maps a score into a probability:

\[ \sigma(z)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-z}} \]
\sigma(z)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-z}}

A neural network stacks transformations:

\[ \mathbf{h}_1=\sigma(W_1\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{b}_1) \]
\mathbf{h}_1=\sigma(W_1\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{b}_1)
\[ \hat{y}=W_2\mathbf{h}_1+\mathbf{b}_2 \]
\hat{y}=W_2\mathbf{h}_1+\mathbf{b}_2

The key shift is representation learning. Instead of only fitting a final output, a neural network learns intermediate hidden representations:

\[ \mathbf{h}=g_\theta(\mathbf{x}) \]
\mathbf{h}=g_\theta(\mathbf{x})

For your work, this is important because hidden representations might encode spatial-affective patterns, policy terrain states, or organisational dynamics that are difficult to hand-define.

The conceptual takeaway:

Machine learning is not magic; it is function fitting through iterative optimisation over parameterised transformations.
10Lesson 10 — Attention and Transformers: Learned Relational Relevance

Attention is one of the central mathematical mechanisms of modern AI. Its core purpose is:

Let each element decide which other elements are relevant to interpreting it.

Given input matrix \(X\), attention creates three learned projections:

\[ Q=XW_Q \]
Q=XW_Q
\[ K=XW_K \]
K=XW_K
\[ V=XW_V \]
V=XW_V

Queries \(Q\) ask: what am I looking for?

Keys \(K\) say: what do I contain or offer?

Values \(V\) say: what information should be passed along if attended to?

The attention equation is:

\[ \mathrm{Attention}(Q,K,V)= \mathrm{softmax}\left( \frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}} \right)V \]
\mathrm{Attention}(Q,K,V)=
\mathrm{softmax}\left(
\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}
\right)V

Step by step:

  • \(QK^T\): compare every query with every key.
  • \(\sqrt{d_k}\): scale scores to stabilise training.
  • \(\mathrm{softmax}\): turn scores into weights.
  • \(V\): mix value vectors using those weights.

So the sentence is:

Compare elements, convert comparisons into relevance weights, then mix information accordingly.

This is not only useful for language. It gives a mathematical intuition for relational interpretation.

In architecture, a threshold might “attend” to visibility, sound, adjacent programme, crowd density, and climatic condition. In PTM, a housing affordability variable may attend to wage growth, interest rates, land supply, fertility, political trust, and transport access.

The deeper idea is that meaning is not stored only inside isolated elements. Meaning is recomposed relationally.

The conceptual takeaway:

Attention is a learned relevance field.
11Lesson 11 — Latent Variables, Variational Inference, and the Free Energy Bridge

A latent variable is a hidden variable that helps explain observed data.

\[ z=\text{latent variable} \]
z=\text{latent variable}
\[ x=\text{observed data} \]
x=\text{observed data}

A generative model says hidden causes generate observations:

\[ p_\theta(x,z)=p_\theta(x\mid z)p(z) \]
p_\theta(x,z)=p_\theta(x\mid z)p(z)

Read:

The joint probability of observation and latent variable factorises into a prior over latent variables and a likelihood that generates observations from latent causes.

The generative story is:

\[ z\sim p(z) \]
z\sim p(z)
\[ x\sim p_\theta(x\mid z) \]
x\sim p_\theta(x\mid z)

But after observing \(x\), we want to infer \(z\):

\[ p_\theta(z\mid x) \]
p_\theta(z\mid x)

By Bayes:

\[ p_\theta(z\mid x)= \frac{p_\theta(x\mid z)p(z)}{p_\theta(x)} \]
p_\theta(z\mid x)=
\frac{p_\theta(x\mid z)p(z)}{p_\theta(x)}

The denominator is often difficult:

\[ p_\theta(x)=\int p_\theta(x\mid z)p(z)\,dz \]
p_\theta(x)=\int p_\theta(x\mid z)p(z)\,dz

So we approximate the posterior:

\[ q_\phi(z\mid x)\approx p_\theta(z\mid x) \]
q_\phi(z\mid x)\approx p_\theta(z\mid x)

The variational objective, the ELBO, is:

\[ \mathcal{L}(\theta,\phi)= \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z\mid x)} [\log p_\theta(x\mid z)] - \mathrm{KL}(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p(z)) \]
\mathcal{L}(\theta,\phi)=
\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z\mid x)}
[\log p_\theta(x\mid z)]
-
\mathrm{KL}(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p(z))

The first term says: infer latents that reconstruct the data well. The second term says: keep the approximate posterior close to the prior.

Free energy is often the negative ELBO:

\[ F=-\mathcal{L} \]
F=-\mathcal{L}

In FEP terms, minimising free energy can be understood as improving the fit between approximate beliefs and the hidden causes of sensory observations.

For architecture, \(z\) might be latent comfort, anxiety, curiosity, social permission, or perceived safety. For PTM, \(z\) might be latent public trust, affordability stress, institutional strain, or social fatigue.

The conceptual takeaway:

When hidden causes are inaccessible, models learn approximate beliefs about them.
12Lesson 12 — Markov Blankets: Boundaries as Informational Structures

A Markov blanket is a mathematical way to describe a boundary, but not merely a physical boundary.

First, conditional independence:

\[ A\perp B\mid C \]
A\perp B\mid C

means:

\(A\) is independent of \(B\), given \(C\).

A Markov blanket of \(X\) satisfies:

\[ X\perp \text{Everything Else}\mid \mathrm{MB}(X) \]
X\perp \text{Everything Else}\mid \mathrm{MB}(X)

This means that once we know the blanket, the rest of the world adds no further information about \(X\).

In FEP discussions, a system is often described using:

  • \(\mu\): internal states
  • \(\eta\): external states
  • \(s\): sensory states
  • \(a\): active states

The blanket is:

\[ b=\{s,a\} \]
b=\{s,a\}

The key conditional independence is:

\[ \mu\perp \eta\mid b \]
\mu\perp \eta\mid b

Read:

Internal states are conditionally independent of external states, given the sensory-active blanket.

This means the inside does not directly access the outside. It senses through sensory states and acts through active states.

This is where the architectural relevance becomes powerful. A wall, door, window, screen, dashboard, threshold, corridor, or institution may participate in a Markov-blanket-like structure if it mediates what can be sensed and how action can occur.

But we must be rigorous. We should not casually say “a wall is a Markov blanket.” More carefully:

A wall or threshold may be modelled as part of a Markov-blanket-like boundary if it mediates conditional dependencies between internal and external states.

For PTM, governance also has blankets. Government internal models do not directly access society. They sense through data, surveys, dashboards, complaints, media, and ground reports. They act through policy, communication, regulation, service redesign, and enforcement.

The danger is a bad blanket: biased sensing and weak action channels.

The conceptual takeaway:

Design operates on the sensory-active boundary conditions through which systems infer, adapt, and act.
13Lesson 13 — Graphs, Networks, and Graph Neural Networks

A graph is a mathematical structure for relations:

\[ G=(V,E) \]
G=(V,E)

where \(V\) is the set of nodes and \(E\) is the set of edges.

In architecture, nodes might be rooms, corridors, stairs, atria, plazas, thresholds. Edges might be adjacency, accessibility, visibility, acoustic leakage, or thermal coupling.

In policy, nodes might be housing, wages, transport, fertility, trust, eldercare, and public sentiment. Edges might be dependencies, causal assumptions, feedback loops, or learned relations.

The adjacency matrix:

\[ A_{ij} \]
A_{ij}

tells whether or how strongly node \(i\) connects to node \(j\).

The neighbourhood of a node is:

\[ \mathcal{N}(i) \]
\mathcal{N}(i)

Graph neural networks update a node’s representation by aggregating information from neighbours:

\[ \mathbf{h}_i^{(k+1)}= \sigma\left( W^{(k)} \sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}(i)} \mathbf{h}_j^{(k)} \right) \]
\mathbf{h}_i^{(k+1)}=
\sigma\left(
W^{(k)}
\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}(i)}
\mathbf{h}_j^{(k)}
\right)

Read:

The next representation of node \(i\) is a learned transformation of the representations of its neighbours.

This is a powerful idea because in many systems, an entity’s meaning depends on its neighbourhood.

A room is not only its area, material, or programme. It is also what it connects to, what it sees, who passes through it, what thresholds it creates, and what flows it mediates.

A policy variable is not only its current value. It is also its dependencies, feedback loops, lags, constraints, and coupling to other variables.

Graph Attention Networks add learned weights:

\[ \mathbf{h}_i^{(k+1)}= \sigma\left( \sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}(i)} \alpha_{ij}W\mathbf{h}_j^{(k)} \right) \]
\mathbf{h}_i^{(k+1)}=
\sigma\left(
\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}(i)}
\alpha_{ij}W\mathbf{h}_j^{(k)}
\right)

where \(\alpha_{ij}\) is the learned importance of neighbour \(j\) for node \(i\).

The conceptual takeaway:

A vector describes what a thing is. A graph describes how things relate. A GNN learns how each thing should be understood through its relational neighbourhood.
14Lesson 14 — State-Space Models and Kalman Filtering

State-space models give a formal language for hidden states evolving over time while observations remain noisy.

The linear state-space model is:

\[ \mathbf{x}_{t+1}=A\mathbf{x}_t+B\mathbf{u}_t+\mathbf{w}_t \]
\mathbf{x}_{t+1}=A\mathbf{x}_t+B\mathbf{u}_t+\mathbf{w}_t
\[ \mathbf{y}_t=C\mathbf{x}_t+\mathbf{v}_t \]
\mathbf{y}_t=C\mathbf{x}_t+\mathbf{v}_t

Decode:

  • \(\mathbf{x}_t\): hidden state at time \(t\)
  • \(\mathbf{y}_t\): observation at time \(t\)
  • \(\mathbf{u}_t\): control input or intervention
  • \(\mathbf{w}_t\): process noise
  • \(\mathbf{v}_t\): observation noise
  • \(A\): state transition matrix
  • \(B\): control matrix
  • \(C\): observation matrix

This distinction between hidden state and observation is central. A policy system’s true latent state is not the same as survey data. A user’s spatial comfort is not the same as movement speed. A student’s understanding is not the same as test performance.

Kalman filtering solves inference for linear Gaussian state-space models. It alternates between prediction and update.

The prediction step estimates:

\[ \hat{\mathbf{x}}_{t|t-1} \]
\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{t|t-1}

meaning the predicted state at time \(t\), given observations up to \(t-1\).

The update uses the innovation or prediction error:

\[ \mathbf{r}_t=\mathbf{y}_t-C\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{t|t-1} \]
\mathbf{r}_t=\mathbf{y}_t-C\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{t|t-1}

The Kalman update is:

\[ \hat{\mathbf{x}}_{t|t} = \hat{\mathbf{x}}_{t|t-1} + K_t\mathbf{r}_t \]
\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{t|t}
=
\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{t|t-1}
+
K_t\mathbf{r}_t

Read:

Updated belief equals predicted belief plus Kalman-gain-weighted prediction error.

This is a concrete mathematical version of predictive processing. The system predicts what it should observe, compares with what it actually observes, and updates belief according to uncertainty.

For PTM, this gives a rigorous structure:

\[ \mathbf{x}_t=\text{latent policy terrain} \]
\mathbf{x}_t=\text{latent policy terrain}
\[ \mathbf{y}_t=\text{observed public indicators} \]
\mathbf{y}_t=\text{observed public indicators}
\[ \mathbf{u}_t=\text{policy intervention} \]
\mathbf{u}_t=\text{policy intervention}

For computational architecture:

\[ \mathbf{x}_t=\text{latent comfort/social state} \]
\mathbf{x}_t=\text{latent comfort/social state}
\[ \mathbf{y}_t=\text{observed behaviour/sensor data} \]
\mathbf{y}_t=\text{observed behaviour/sensor data}
\[ \mathbf{u}_t=\text{design or environmental adjustment} \]
\mathbf{u}_t=\text{design or environmental adjustment}

The conceptual takeaway:

State-space models describe hidden states evolving under uncertainty, observed through noisy signals, and updated through prediction error.
15Lesson 15 — Reinforcement Learning and Active Decision-Making

Reinforcement learning moves from inferring the world to acting in the world.

The basic loop is:

\[ s_t\rightarrow a_t\rightarrow r_t,s_{t+1} \]
s_t\rightarrow a_t\rightarrow r_t,s_{t+1}

At time \(t\), the agent is in state \(s_t\), takes action \(a_t\), receives reward \(r_t\), and the environment moves to \(s_{t+1}\).

A policy is:

\[ \pi(a\mid s) \]
\pi(a\mid s)

Read:

The probability of choosing action \(a\), given state \(s\).

The transition dynamics are:

\[ P(s'\mid s,a) \]
P(s'\mid s,a)

meaning the probability of next state \(s'\) after action \(a\) in state \(s\).

The return is cumulative discounted reward:

\[ G_t=r_t+\gamma r_{t+1}+\gamma^2r_{t+2}+\dots \]
G_t=r_t+\gamma r_{t+1}+\gamma^2r_{t+2}+\dots

where \(\gamma\) is the discount factor. If \(\gamma\) is low, the agent is short-termist. If \(\gamma\) is close to 1, the agent values long-term outcomes.

The action-value function is:

\[ Q^\pi(s,a)= \mathbb{E}_\pi[G_t\mid s_t=s,a_t=a] \]
Q^\pi(s,a)=
\mathbb{E}_\pi[G_t\mid s_t=s,a_t=a]

Read:

How good is it to take action \(a\) in state \(s\), then follow policy \(\pi\)?

Q-learning updates:

\[ Q(s,a)\leftarrow Q(s,a)+\alpha[ r+\gamma\max_{a'}Q(s',a')-Q(s,a)] \]
Q(s,a)\leftarrow
Q(s,a)+\alpha[
r+\gamma\max_{a'}Q(s',a')-Q(s,a)]

The bracketed term is temporal-difference error:

\[ \delta=r+\gamma\max_{a'}Q(s',a')-Q(s,a) \]
\delta=r+\gamma\max_{a'}Q(s',a')-Q(s,a)

In policy and architecture, RL is conceptually powerful but ethically dangerous if reward is poorly specified. If architecture optimises dwell time only, it may produce confusion or entrapment. If policy optimises complaint reduction only, it may suppress reporting rather than solve problems.

This links to your governance instincts:

Action requires value specification, not merely prediction.
16Lesson 16 — Information Theory: Surprise, Entropy, KL, and Mutual Information

Information theory measures uncertainty, surprise, compression, and dependence.

The surprise of an event is:

\[ I(x)=-\log p(x) \]
I(x)=-\log p(x)

If \(p(x)\) is high, surprise is low. If \(p(x)\) is low, surprise is high.

Entropy is expected surprise:

\[ H(X)=-\sum_x p(x)\log p(x) \]
H(X)=-\sum_x p(x)\log p(x)

Low entropy means the outcome is predictable. High entropy means possibilities are spread out.

Cross-entropy is:

\[ H(p,q)=-\sum_x p(x)\log q(x) \]
H(p,q)=-\sum_x p(x)\log q(x)

It measures expected surprise when using model distribution \(q\) for data generated by true distribution \(p\).

KL divergence is:

\[ \mathrm{KL}(p\|q)= \sum_x p(x)\log\frac{p(x)}{q(x)} \]
\mathrm{KL}(p\|q)=
\sum_x p(x)\log\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}

It measures extra surprise from using \(q\) instead of \(p\). It is not symmetric:

\[ \mathrm{KL}(p\|q)\neq \mathrm{KL}(q\|p) \]
\mathrm{KL}(p\|q)\neq \mathrm{KL}(q\|p)

Mutual information is:

\[ I(X;Y)=H(X)-H(X\mid Y) \]
I(X;Y)=H(X)-H(X\mid Y)

Read:

How much does knowing \(Y\) reduce uncertainty about \(X\)?

This is useful for your work because you often care about whether one signal truly tells us something about another hidden state. For example:

\[ I(\text{visibility};\text{dwell time}) \]
I(\text{visibility};\text{dwell time})

asks how much visibility reduces uncertainty about dwell time.

\[ I(\text{dashboard signal};\text{actual social condition}) \]
I(\text{dashboard signal};\text{actual social condition})

asks how much a governance dashboard actually knows about lived reality.

The conceptual takeaway:

Information theory gives mathematical precision to uncertainty, signal, surprise, relevance, and dependence.
17Lesson 17 — Bayesian Modelling and Hierarchical Models

Bayesian modelling applies Bayes’ theorem to parameters:

\[ p(\theta\mid D)= \frac{p(D\mid \theta)p(\theta)}{p(D)} \]
p(\theta\mid D)=
\frac{p(D\mid \theta)p(\theta)}{p(D)}

Here \(\theta\) represents model parameters and \(D\) represents data.

Unlike ordinary fitting, Bayesian modelling does not only produce one best estimate. It gives a distribution over plausible parameter values:

\[ p(\theta\mid D) \]
p(\theta\mid D)

This is important because it keeps uncertainty visible.

A Bayesian regression model may be written:

\[ y_i\sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_i,\sigma^2) \]
y_i\sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_i,\sigma^2)
\[ \mu_i=\alpha+\beta x_i \]
\mu_i=\alpha+\beta x_i
\[ \alpha\sim \mathcal{N}(0,10^2) \]
\alpha\sim \mathcal{N}(0,10^2)
\[ \beta\sim \mathcal{N}(0,5^2) \]
\beta\sim \mathcal{N}(0,5^2)

This is a generative story:

  • Choose plausible intercept \(\alpha\).
  • Choose plausible slope \(\beta\).
  • For each data point, compute expected value \(\mu_i\).
  • Generate observed \(y_i\) around \(\mu_i\) with noise.

Hierarchical models allow parameters to vary across groups while sharing information. For example:

\[ y_{ij}\sim \mathcal{N}(\alpha_j+\beta_jx_{ij},\sigma^2) \]
y_{ij}\sim \mathcal{N}(\alpha_j+\beta_jx_{ij},\sigma^2)
\[ \alpha_j\sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_\alpha,\sigma_\alpha^2) \]
\alpha_j\sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_\alpha,\sigma_\alpha^2)

Here group \(j\) has its own intercept, but group intercepts are drawn from a shared population distribution.

This creates partial pooling. Small groups borrow strength from the whole dataset; large groups can stand more independently. This is extremely useful in design, education, policy, and urban research where data are uneven across sites or cohorts.

The conceptual takeaway:

Bayesian modelling turns assumptions into visible probabilistic structure.
18Lesson 18 — Causal Inference: Correlation, Intervention, and \(do(\cdot)\)

Causal inference begins with one critical distinction:

\[ P(Y\mid X=x) \]
P(Y\mid X=x)

versus:

\[ P(Y\mid do(X=x)) \]
P(Y\mid do(X=x))

The first means:

What is \(Y\) like among cases where we observe \(X=x\)?

The second means:

What would \(Y\) be if we intervened to set \(X=x\)?

These are not the same.

For example, shaded spaces may have higher dwell time. But that does not prove shade causes dwell time. Shaded spaces may also be better located, better maintained, near cafés, safer, or more socially active.

A confounder has this structure:

\[ X\leftarrow Z\rightarrow Y \]
X\leftarrow Z\rightarrow Y

where \(Z\) affects both \(X\) and \(Y\).

Potential outcomes define:

\[ Y_i(1) \]
Y_i(1)

outcome for unit \(i\) under treatment, and:

\[ Y_i(0) \]
Y_i(0)

outcome for unit \(i\) under control.

The individual treatment effect is:

\[ \tau_i=Y_i(1)-Y_i(0) \]
\tau_i=Y_i(1)-Y_i(0)

But we cannot observe both potential outcomes for the same unit at the same time. This is the fundamental problem of causal inference.

The average treatment effect is:

\[ ATE=\mathbb{E}[Y(1)-Y(0)] \]
ATE=\mathbb{E}[Y(1)-Y(0)]

If a sufficient set of confounders \(Z\) is observed, the backdoor adjustment is:

\[ P(Y\mid do(X=x))= \sum_zP(Y\mid X=x,Z=z)P(Z=z) \]
P(Y\mid do(X=x))=
\sum_zP(Y\mid X=x,Z=z)P(Z=z)

For PTM, this distinction is crucial. A policy terrain model should not only predict:

\[ P(\text{stress}\mid \text{current indicators}) \]
P(\text{stress}\mid \text{current indicators})

It should also ask:

\[ P(\text{future stress}\mid do(\text{intervention})) \]
P(\text{future stress}\mid do(\text{intervention}))

The conceptual takeaway:

Prediction asks what patterns exist. Causality asks what would happen under intervention.
19Lesson 19 — Econometrics and Statistical Identification

Econometrics is often misunderstood as regression. At its best, it is the discipline of identifying meaningful causal or structural relationships from imperfect real-world data.

The basic regression equation is:

\[ Y_i=\alpha+\beta X_i+\epsilon_i \]
Y_i=\alpha+\beta X_i+\epsilon_i

where \(Y_i\) is outcome, \(X_i\) is predictor or treatment, \(\alpha\) is intercept, \(\beta\) is coefficient, and \(\epsilon_i\) is error term.

The crucial assumption is:

\[ \mathbb{E}[\epsilon_i\mid X_i]=0 \]
\mathbb{E}[\epsilon_i\mid X_i]=0

Read:

The expected error is zero given \(X_i\).

This means unobserved factors in \(\epsilon_i\) are not systematically related to \(X_i\). If they are related, then \(X_i\) is endogenous and \(\beta\) may be biased.

Omitted variable bias occurs when a missing variable affects \(Y\) and is correlated with \(X\).

Panel data models use units over time:

\[ Y_{it}=\alpha_i+\lambda_t+\beta D_{it}+\epsilon_{it} \]
Y_{it}=\alpha_i+\lambda_t+\beta D_{it}+\epsilon_{it}

Here \(\alpha_i\) controls for unit fixed effects and \(\lambda_t\) controls for time fixed effects.

Difference-in-differences estimates treatment effects by comparing changes in treated and control groups over time. Its key assumption is parallel trends.

Instrumental variables use a variable \(Z_i\) that affects treatment \(X_i\) but affects outcome \(Y_i\) only through \(X_i\). This is powerful but assumption-heavy.

For your work, econometrics provides discipline. If PTM or computational architecture models show a pattern, econometrics asks:

  • Is this predictive or causal?
  • What is the identification strategy?
  • What confounders exist?
  • What is the counterfactual?
  • What assumptions justify the claim?

The conceptual takeaway:

Regression is a tool. Identification is the argument.
20Lesson 20 — Dynamical Systems, Stability, and Bifurcation

Dynamical systems study behaviour over time.

Discrete time:

\[ x_{t+1}=f(x_t) \]
x_{t+1}=f(x_t)

Continuous time:

\[ \frac{dx}{dt}=f(x) \]
\frac{dx}{dt}=f(x)

or:

\[ \dot{x}=f(x) \]
\dot{x}=f(x)

\[ f(x^*)=0 \]
f(x^*)=0

A fixed point in discrete time satisfies:

\[ x^*=f(x^*) \]
x^*=f(x^*)

Stability asks whether nearby states move toward or away from equilibrium.

For one-dimensional continuous systems:

\[ f'(x^*)<0 \]
f'(x^*)<0

means locally stable.

For one-dimensional discrete systems:

\[ |f'(x^*)|<1 \]
|f'(x^*)|<1

means locally stable.

For multivariable systems:

\[ \dot{\mathbf{x}}=\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}) \]
\dot{\mathbf{x}}=\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})

\[ J=\frac{\partial \mathbf{f}}{\partial \mathbf{x}} \]
J=\frac{\partial \mathbf{f}}{\partial \mathbf{x}}

\[ J\mathbf{v}=\lambda\mathbf{v} \]

\[ \mathrm{Re}(\lambda_i)<0 \]
\mathrm{Re}(\lambda_i)<0

Dynamical systems are the mathematics of behaviour over time.
21Lesson 21 — Complexity Science Proper: Emergence, Scaling, Networks, and Agents

Complexity science studies systems where many interacting parts generate collective behaviour that is not obvious from the parts alone.

The core relation is:

\[ \text{micro rules}+\text{interactions}\rightarrow\text{macro patterns} \]
\text{micro rules}+\text{interactions}\rightarrow\text{macro patterns}

A complicated system can often be decomposed. A complex system evolves through interaction, feedback, adaptation, and emergence.

An interaction model may include terms like:

\[ Y=\beta_0+\beta_1X_1+\beta_2X_2+\beta_3X_1X_2+\epsilon \]

\[ s_i(t+1)=f_i(s_i(t),\mathcal{N}_i(t),E(t)) \]

\[ G=(V,E) \]
G=(V,E)

\[ P(k)\sim k^{-\alpha} \]

\[ Y\sim N^\beta \]

\[ \log Y=\log Y_0+\beta\log N \]

For PTM, complexity science reframes policy as evolving terrain rather than linear cause-effect chain. For computational architecture, it reframes buildings and cities as systems that generate behavioural and affective dynamics.

The conceptual takeaway:

The system is not the sum of its parts; it is the evolving pattern of relations among parts.

23Lesson 23 — How to Read Mathematical Papers Without Panic

Mathematical papers are not written like gentle textbooks. They compress a research contribution for readers already inside the discourse.   • Theory paper
  • Modelling paper
  • Machine learning paper
  • Empirical/econometric paper
  • Causal inference paper
  • Complexity/simulation paper
  • FEP/active inference paper

Identify the role of each equation:

  • Definition
  • Assumption
  • Model
  • Objective function
  • Update rule
  • Estimator
  • Result
  • Causal claim

\[ \theta^*=\arg\min_\theta L(\theta) \]
\theta^*=\arg\min_\theta L(\theta)

is an objective.

\[ \theta\leftarrow\theta-\eta\nabla L \]

\[ \epsilon_i\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2) \]
\epsilon_i\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)

means:

  • What is observed?
  • What is hidden?
  • What is being learned?
  • What is being assumed?

Do not ask first, “How do I solve this?” Ask, “What is this equation trying to say about the world?”
