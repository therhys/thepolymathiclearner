Layer 00

0. The Whole Ladder

The conceptual path is:

space → dimension → curvature → topology → manifold → metric → complex structure → Kähler geometry → Chern class → Ricci-flatness → Calabi–Yau manifold

The mathematical path is:

sets → coordinates → vectors → functions → derivatives → tangent spaces → metric tensor → curvature tensor → topology → manifolds → complex coordinates → differential forms → Kähler form → Chern classes → Ricci form → Calabi–Yau condition
Diagram 1. A high-level map of the ascent: manifold structure first, then metric, complex structure, Kähler compatibility, and finally the Calabi–Yau condition.
The teaching strategy is cumulative: every new symbol appears because the previous layer has made it necessary.
Layer 01

1. Spaces as Sets

At the most basic level, a mathematical space begins as a set: a collection of objects.

\[ X=\{\text{objects}\} \]

The real line, the plane, and three-dimensional space are:

\[ \mathbb{R}=\{\text{all real numbers}\} \] \[ \mathbb{R}^2=\{(x,y):x,y\in\mathbb{R}\} \] \[ \mathbb{R}^3=\{(x,y,z):x,y,z\in\mathbb{R}\} \]

In general:

\[ \mathbb{R}^n=\{(x_1,x_2,\ldots,x_n):x_i\in\mathbb{R}\} \]
Diagram 2. Dimension as coordinate count: a point in ℝn is a tuple of n numbers.
A point in \( \mathbb{R}^n \) is simply a list of \( n \) numbers. Higher dimensions are not mystical; they are additional coordinates, or additional degrees of freedom.

Layer 02

2. Distance in Flat Space

In ordinary flat geometry, distance comes from Pythagoras.

\[ p=(x_1,y_1),\qquad q=(x_2,y_2), \] \[ d(p,q)=\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2} \]

In three dimensions:

\[ d(p,q)=\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2+(z_2-z_1)^2} \]

In \(n\) dimensions:

\[ d(p,q)=\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i-y_i)^2} \]
Diagram 3. Euclidean distance as coordinate difference accumulated through the Pythagorean rule.
This is the default flat geometry of \( \mathbb{R}^n \). Later, curved spaces will replace this simple distance rule with a metric tensor.

Layer 03

3. Vectors and Dot Products

A vector is a direction with magnitude. In \( \mathbb{R}^n \):

\[ v=(v_1,v_2,\ldots,v_n) \]

The length of a vector is:

\[ |v|=\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n v_i^2} \]

The dot product of two vectors is:

\[ u\cdot v=\sum_{i=1}^n u_i v_i \]

The dot product also gives angles:

\[ u\cdot v=|u||v|\cos\theta \]
Diagram 4. The dot product is the geometry of directional agreement.
Layer 04

4. Functions and Derivatives

A function maps one space to another:

\[ f:X\to Y \]

For example:

\[ f:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R},\qquad f(x)=x^2 \] \[ f:\mathbb{R}^2\to\mathbb{R},\qquad f(x,y)=x^2+y^2 \]

If \(f(x,y)\) gives height, then \(z=f(x,y)\) describes a surface.

\[ z=x^2+y^2 \qquad z=x^2-y^2 \]

For a function \(f(x)\), the derivative \( \frac{df}{dx} \) measures rate of change.

\[ f(x)=x^2,\qquad \frac{df}{dx}=2x \]

For \(f(x,y)\), we use partial derivatives:

\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x},\qquad \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \]

The gradient is:

\[ \nabla f = \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}, \ldots, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} \right) \]
Diagram 5. A derivative is local change. A gradient is the direction of steepest increase on a scalar field.
Curved geometry depends on understanding local change. To understand manifolds, we need calculus locally.
Layer 05

5. Parametric Surfaces

A surface can be described using internal parameters:

\[ X(u,v)=\big(x(u,v),y(u,v),z(u,v)\big) \]

A sphere of radius \(r\) can be parametrised by:

\[ X(\theta,\phi)= \big( r\sin\phi\cos\theta,\, r\sin\phi\sin\theta,\, r\cos\phi \big) \] \[ 0\leq\theta<2\pi,\qquad 0\leq\phi\leq\pi \]
Diagram 6. A parametrisation gives the sphere its own internal coordinate system.
The sphere is two-dimensional even though it sits in three-dimensional space, because each point on the sphere only needs two internal coordinates: \( (\theta,\phi) \).

Layer 06

6. Tangent Vectors and Tangent Spaces

For a parametrised surface \(X(u,v)\), we differentiate with respect to \(u\) and \(v\):

\[ X_u=\frac{\partial X}{\partial u},\qquad X_v=\frac{\partial X}{\partial v} \]

These are tangent vectors. The tangent plane at a point is spanned by them:

\[ T_pM=\operatorname{span}\{X_u,X_v\} \]

For an \(n\)-dimensional manifold:

\[ T_pM\cong\mathbb{R}^n \]
Diagram 7. The tangent space is the manifold’s local linearisation at a point.
curved space globally linear algebra locally

Layer 07

7. Manifolds and Charts

A manifold \(M\) is a space that locally looks like Euclidean space. For every point \(p\in M\), there is a neighbourhood \(U\) and a coordinate map:

\[ \varphi:U\to\mathbb{R}^n \]

The map \( \varphi \) is called a chart. A collection of charts is an atlas:

\[ \{(U_i,\varphi_i)\} \]

If two charts overlap, the transition map is:

\[ \varphi_j\circ\varphi_i^{-1}: \varphi_i(U_i\cap U_j)\to \varphi_j(U_i\cap U_j) \]
Diagram 8. A manifold is covered by local coordinate charts; the overlap must transform smoothly.
A manifold is like Earth: each map is locally useful, but no single flat map captures the whole world perfectly.
Layer 08

8. Metric Tensor

In flat Euclidean space, the dot product is:

\[ u\cdot v=\sum_i u_i v_i \]

On a curved manifold, we replace this with a metric tensor. At each point \(p\), the metric takes two tangent vectors and gives a number:

\[ g_p:T_pM\times T_pM\to\mathbb{R} \]

In coordinates:

\[ g=\sum_{i,j} g_{ij}\,dx^i dx^j \] \[ ds^2=\sum_{i,j} g_{ij}\,dx^i dx^j \]

Euclidean example:

\[ ds^2=dx^2+dy^2,\qquad g= \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0\\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \]

Sphere example:

\[ ds^2=r^2\,d\phi^2+r^2\sin^2\phi\,d\theta^2 \]
Diagram 9. A metric tells the space how to measure. Change the metric, and the geometry changes.
The metric is the space’s internal measuring rule. It tells the manifold how to measure length, angle, area, and shortest paths.
Layer 09

9. Geodesics and Curvature

In flat space, the shortest path is a straight line. On a curved manifold, the shortest path is called a geodesic.

\[ \frac{d^2x^k}{dt^2}+\Gamma^k_{ij}\frac{dx^i}{dt}\frac{dx^j}{dt}=0 \]

The symbols \( \Gamma^k_{ij} \) are Christoffel symbols. They describe how coordinates bend and how tangent vectors change as we move.

The full curvature of a manifold is encoded in the Riemann curvature tensor:

\[ R^p{}_{sij} \]

Ricci curvature is a contraction of the Riemann curvature tensor:

\[ \operatorname{Ric}_{ij}=R^k{}_{ikj} \]

Scalar curvature is another contraction:

\[ R=g^{ij}\operatorname{Ric}_{ij} \]
Diagram 10. Flat, positively curved, and negatively curved spaces differ in how their geodesics behave.
Calabi–Yau manifolds are Ricci-flat: \(\operatorname{Ric}_{ij}=0\). But this does not necessarily mean the full Riemann tensor vanishes: \(R^p{}_{sij}\neq 0\). A Calabi–Yau manifold can be Ricci-flat while still having rich internal curvature.

10. Topology and Holes

Topology studies properties unchanged by continuous deformation. One simple invariant is the Euler characteristic:

\[ \chi=V-E+F \]

For a genus \(g\) surface:

\[ \chi=2-2g \]

Layer 11

11. Complex Numbers as Geometry

A complex number is:

\[ z=x+iy,\qquad i^2=-1 \]

The complex plane is:

\[ \mathbb{C}=\{z=x+iy:x,y\in\mathbb{R}\},\qquad \mathbb{C}\cong\mathbb{R}^2 \]

Multiplication by \(i\) rotates by \(90^\circ\):

\[ i(x+iy)=ix+i^2y=-y+ix \] \[ (x,y)\mapsto (-y,x) \]
Diagram 12. The imaginary unit i acts as a geometric rotation operator on the plane.
A complex structure on a real manifold is, roughly, a smoothly varying way to rotate tangent vectors by \(90^\circ\).

Layer 12

12. Complex Structure

A complex structure is an operator:

\[ J:TM\to TM \]

acting on tangent vectors such that:

\[ J^2=-I \]

If \(v\) is a tangent vector, \(Jv\) is the vector rotated by \(90^\circ\) in the complex direction. Doing it twice gives a \(180^\circ\) rotation:

\[ J^2v=-v \]
Diagram 13. The operator J generalises multiplication by i from the complex plane to each tangent space.
A real 6-dimensional manifold with a complex structure may be understood as complex 3-dimensional: \(\mathbb{R}^6\cong\mathbb{C}^3\).
Layer 13

13. Complex Manifolds

A complex manifold of complex dimension \(n\) is locally modelled on:

\[ \mathbb{C}^n \]

Its coordinates are:

\[ (z^1,z^2,\ldots,z^n),\qquad z^k=x^k+iy^k \]

Therefore:

\[ \dim_{\mathbb{C}}M=n\quad\Longrightarrow\quad \dim_{\mathbb{R}}M=2n \]

Complex dimension 1

\(\mathbb{C}\cong\mathbb{R}^2\)

Complex dimension 2

\(\mathbb{C}^2\cong\mathbb{R}^4\)

Complex dimension 3

\(\mathbb{C}^3\cong\mathbb{R}^6\)

A complex function \(f:\mathbb{C}\to\mathbb{C}\) can be written:

\[ f(z)=u(x,y)+iv(x,y),\qquad z=x+iy \]

The Cauchy–Riemann equations are:

\[ \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}=\frac{\partial v}{\partial y},\qquad \frac{\partial u}{\partial y}=-\frac{\partial v}{\partial x} \]
Complex manifolds are glued together using holomorphic transition functions. This makes complex geometry much more rigid than ordinary smooth geometry.
Layer 14

14. Differential Forms

Differential forms are objects that can be integrated over curves, surfaces, and higher-dimensional regions.

\[ \alpha = f\,dx + g\,dy \qquad \beta=h\,dx\wedge dy \]

The wedge product \( \wedge \) is antisymmetric:

\[ dx\wedge dy=-dy\wedge dx,\qquad dx\wedge dx=0 \]

The exterior derivative is written \(d\), and satisfies:

\[ d^2=0 \]

A symplectic form is a closed, non-degenerate 2-form \( \omega \):

\[ d\omega=0 \]

In phase space coordinates \((q,p)\), the canonical symplectic form is:

\[ \omega=dq\wedge dp \]
Diagram 14. Differential forms are the native language for integration and geometric structure on manifolds.
Kähler geometry combines metric geometry, complex geometry, and symplectic geometry into one compatible structure.
Layer 15

15. Kähler Geometry

A Kähler manifold has three mutually compatible structures:

\[ g=\text{metric},\qquad J=\text{complex structure},\qquad \omega=\text{Kähler form} \]

They satisfy:

\[ \omega(X,Y) = g(JX,Y),\qquad d\omega=0 \]

In complex coordinates \( (z^1,\ldots,z^n) \), a Kähler metric can be written as:

\[ ds^2=2\sum_{i,j}g_{i\bar{j}}\,dz^i\,d\bar{z}^j \]

The Kähler form is:

\[ \omega=i\sum_{i,j}g_{i\bar{j}}\,dz^i\wedge d\bar{z}^j \]

Different references use a slightly different normalisation convention for the Kähler form. With the factor of 2 above in the metric, one also sees:

\[ \omega=\frac{i}{2}\sum_{i,j}g_{i\bar{j}}\,dz^i\wedge d\bar{z}^j \]

Both conventions are correct; the relation \( \omega(X,Y)=g(JX,Y) \) fixes the normalisation once the metric convention is chosen.

Locally, the metric can often be derived from a scalar function \(K\), the Kähler potential:

\[ g_{i\bar{j}}= \frac{\partial^2 K}{\partial z^i \partial \bar{z}^j} \]
Diagram 15. Kähler geometry is the compatibility of metric, complex structure, and symplectic form.
This is beautiful because a whole metric can locally come from one potential function. It is like deriving a landscape from a height field, but in complex geometry.

Layer 16

16. Ricci Form

On a Kähler manifold, Ricci curvature can be expressed using the Ricci form \( \rho \).

\[ \rho=-i\,\partial\bar{\partial}\log\det(g_{i\bar{j}}) \]

Ricci-flatness means:

\[ \rho=0,\qquad \operatorname{Ric}_{i\bar{j}}=0 \]
Ricci-flatness means the complex metric has a special balance. The space may still curve in complicated ways, but its averaged complex curvature cancels.
Layer 17

17. Chern Classes

Chern classes measure the twisting of complex vector bundles. The first Chern class is:

\[ c_1 \]

For a complex manifold, the first Chern class of its tangent bundle is:

\[ c_1(TM) \]

often written simply as:

\[ c_1(M) \]

On a Kähler manifold, the Ricci form represents the first Chern class:

\[ \left[\frac{\rho}{2\pi}\right]=c_1(M) \]

When:

\[ c_1(M)=0 \]

the relevant complex twisting vanishes topologically.

Diagram 16. The first Chern class is where local complex curvature becomes global topological content.
The Ricci form is geometric. The first Chern class is topological. This equation says that Ricci curvature encodes a topological twisting class.
Layer 18

18. Canonical Bundle

For a complex manifold of complex dimension \(n\), the canonical bundle is the bundle of holomorphic \(n\)-forms:

\[ K_M=\Lambda^n(T^{*1,0}M) \]

A section of this bundle is a holomorphic volume form \( \Omega \). Locally:

\[ \Omega=f(z)\,dz^1\wedge dz^2\wedge\cdots\wedge dz^n \]

For a Calabi–Yau manifold, there exists a nowhere-vanishing holomorphic volume form:

\[ \Omega\neq 0 \quad \text{everywhere} \]

The condition \( c_1(M)=0 \) is related to the canonical bundle being trivial:

\[ K_M\cong \mathcal{O}_M \]
Layer 19

19. Calabi’s Conjecture and Yau’s Theorem

Let \(M\) be a compact Kähler manifold. If:

\[ c_1(M)=0 \]

then there exists a unique Ricci-flat Kähler metric in each Kähler class.

\[ c_1(M)=0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \exists \text{ Ricci-flat Kähler metric} \]
Diagram 17. Yau’s theorem turns a topological vanishing condition into the existence of a special metric.
Calabi asked whether the right topological balance permits a perfectly balanced geometric metric. Yau proved that it does.
Layer 20

20. Calabi–Yau Manifold: Definitions

Common Geometric Definition

A Calabi–Yau manifold is a compact Kähler manifold \(M\) with:

\[ c_1(M)=0 \]

By Yau’s theorem, such a manifold admits a Ricci-flat Kähler metric:

\[ \operatorname{Ric}(g)=0 \]

Holonomy Definition

A stricter definition says that a Calabi–Yau \(n\)-fold is a compact Kähler manifold with holonomy group:

\[ \operatorname{Hol}(g)=SU(n) \]

or contained in:

\[ \operatorname{Hol}(g)\subseteq SU(n) \]

Holomorphic Volume Form Definition

Another formulation says that a Calabi–Yau \(n\)-fold has a nowhere-vanishing holomorphic \(n\)-form:

\[ \Omega\in H^0(M,K_M),\qquad \Omega\neq 0\ \text{everywhere} \]
A Calabi–Yau manifold is a finite, boundaryless complex geometric space where the metric, complex structure, and volume structure fit together so perfectly that the space’s averaged curvature vanishes, even though its internal geometry may still be highly intricate.

Layer 21

21. Calabi–Yau Threefold

The most famous case in string theory is a Calabi–Yau threefold:

\[ \dim_{\mathbb{C}}M=3 \]

Therefore:

\[ \dim_{\mathbb{R}}M=6 \]

Local complex coordinates are:

\[ (z^1,z^2,z^3),\qquad z^1=x^1+iy^1,\ z^2=x^2+iy^2,\ z^3=x^3+iy^3 \]

So real coordinates are:

\[ (x^1,y^1,x^2,y^2,x^3,y^3) \]

The holomorphic volume form locally looks like:

\[ \Omega=dz^1\wedge dz^2\wedge dz^3 \]

The Kähler form looks like:

\[ \omega=i\sum_{i,j}g_{i\bar{j}}\,dz^i\wedge d\bar{z}^j \]

The Ricci-flat condition is:

\[ \operatorname{Ric}_{i\bar{j}}=0,\qquad \rho=-i\partial\bar{\partial}\log\det(g_{i\bar{j}})=0 \]
Diagram 18. A Calabi–Yau threefold lives most naturally as a complex three-dimensional space.
Layer 22

22. Examples

Example 1: Complex Torus

A complex torus is:

\[ \mathbb{C}^n/\Lambda \]

where \( \Lambda \) is a lattice. In one complex dimension:

\[ \mathbb{C}/(\mathbb{Z}+\tau\mathbb{Z}) \]

A real two-dimensional torus can be represented as:

\[ \mathbb{R}^2/\mathbb{Z}^2 \]
Diagram 19. The torus is a local-flat, global-nontrivial space built by identifying boundary directions.
Complex tori have \(c_1=0\) and flat Ricci curvature, making them simple illustrative examples. Strictly speaking, their holonomy is contained in \(U(n)\) rather than exactly \(SU(n)\), so they are sometimes called Calabi–Yau in the broad sense but not the strict holonomy sense.

Example 2: The Quintic Threefold

A famous Calabi–Yau threefold is the quintic hypersurface in complex projective space:

\[ X= \left\{ [z_0:z_1:z_2:z_3:z_4]\in\mathbb{CP}^4: z_0^5+z_1^5+z_2^5+z_3^5+z_4^5=0 \right\} \]

Since \( \mathbb{CP}^4 \) has complex dimension 4, and one equation cuts the dimension down by 1:

\[ \dim_{\mathbb{C}}X=4-1=3,\qquad \dim_{\mathbb{R}}X=6 \]

For a hypersurface of degree \(d\) in \( \mathbb{CP}^n \), the canonical bundle relation gives:

\[ K_X=\mathcal{O}(d-n-1) \]

Here \( d=5 \) and \( n=4 \), so:

\[ d-n-1=5-4-1=0,\qquad K_X=\mathcal{O}_X \]

Therefore the canonical bundle is trivial:

\[ c_1(X)=0 \]
Diagram 20. The quintic threefold is one of the canonical examples of a Calabi–Yau manifold.
The quintic is a six-real-dimensional hidden shape defined by a balanced degree-5 equation in complex projective space.
Layer 23

23. The Monge–Ampère Equation

Yau’s proof involves solving a complex Monge–Ampère equation. One seeks a new Kähler metric in the same Kähler class.

If \( \omega \) is an initial Kähler form, we look for:

\[ \omega'=\omega+i\partial\bar{\partial}\varphi \]

where \( \varphi \) is a scalar potential. The equation takes the form:

\[ \left(\omega+i\partial\bar{\partial}\varphi\right)^n=e^F\omega^n \]

For the Calabi–Yau case, the desired condition is:

\[ \operatorname{Ric}(\omega')=0 \]
We are adjusting the geometry by changing the potential \( \varphi \), while staying in the same broad Kähler class, until the Ricci curvature satisfies the desired condition.
