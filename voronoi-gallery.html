Five geometries · One idea

The Voronoi
Universe

A single question — which seed owns each point in space? — gives rise to five entirely different geometries depending on how you measure distance, weight the seeds, or curve the surface.

Dual graph · Circumcircles

Delaunay
Triangulation

The exact dual of Voronoi — connect every pair of seeds whose cells share a border. The result is the triangulation that maximises the minimum angle, avoiding sliver triangles. Drag seeds to watch edges flip in real time. Toggle circumcircles to see the defining property: no seed lies inside any triangle's circumcircle.

Defining property
$\text{For every triangle } \triangle(p_i, p_j, p_k) \text{ in } \mathcal{D}, \text{ no other seed } p_l \text{ lies inside its circumcircle } C(p_i,p_j,p_k)$
What is it? The dual graph of the Voronoi diagram — connect every pair of seeds whose Voronoi cells share a border edge.
Circumcircle condition A triangulation is Delaunay if and only if no seed lies strictly inside any triangle's circumcircle. This uniquely determines the triangulation.
Edge flip rule Given quadrilateral $ABCD$ with diagonal $AC$: flip to $BD$ if $p_D$ lies inside the circumcircle of $\triangle ABC$. Repeat until stable.
Max-min angle Among all triangulations of the same point set, Delaunay maximises the minimum angle — it avoids slivers. Optimal for interpolation and mesh generation.
Duality means every Voronoi vertex is a circumcenter of a Delaunay triangle. The two structures are two views of the same underlying combinatorial object.

02
Weighted Voronoi · Bubbles

Power
Diagram

Each seed has a weight (radius). The boundary shifts toward the weaker seed — larger seeds claim more territory. The boundary equation becomes ‖x − pᵢ‖² − wᵢ = ‖x − pⱼ‖² − wⱼ. Used in foam simulation, protein structure analysis, and urban planning. Drag seeds; scroll-wheel to resize them.

Distance function · Boundary condition
$\pi(x, p_i) = \|x - p_i\|^2 - w_i \qquad \text{(power distance)}$
$V_i^{\text{pow}} = \bigl\{\, x \in \mathbb{R}^2 \;\big|\; \pi(x,p_i) \le \pi(x,p_j) \;\text{ for all } j \ne i \,\bigr\}$
What changed? Each seed $p_i$ carries a weight $w_i \ge 0$ (visualised as its radius $r_i = \sqrt{w_i}$). Subtract it from the squared distance before comparing.
Boundary equation The boundary between $p_i$ and $p_j$ is where $\|x-p_i\|^2 - w_i = \|x-p_j\|^2 - w_j$ — still a straight line, but shifted toward the lighter seed.
Equal weights When all $w_i$ are equal, the $w$ terms cancel and you recover the standard Voronoi diagram exactly.
Applications Protein pocket detection, foam/soap-bubble geometry, optimal transport, and capacity-weighted urban service areas.
A seed can even have zero territory if it is too light relative to its neighbours — it gets entirely swallowed. Watch this happen when you scroll-wheel a seed down to a tiny radius.

03
L¹ metric · City-block distance

Manhattan
Distance Voronoi

Swap the Euclidean ruler for city-block distance: d(x,p) = |x₁−p₁| + |x₂−p₂|. Cells become rotated squares and diamonds rather than polygons. Slide between L¹ (Manhattan), L² (Euclidean), and L∞ (Chebyshev) to morph the geometry continuously.

The Lᵖ family of metrics
$d_p(x, q) = \left( |x_1 - q_1|^p + |x_2 - q_2|^p \right)^{1/p}$
L¹ · Manhattan ($p=1$) $d_1 = |x_1-q_1| + |x_2-q_2|$. Distance is the sum of axis-aligned steps — like navigating a city grid. Cells become rotated squares (diamonds).
L² · Euclidean ($p=2$) $d_2 = \sqrt{(x_1-q_1)^2+(x_2-q_2)^2}$. The familiar straight-line distance. Cells are convex polygons with straight edges.
L∞ · Chebyshev ($p\to\infty$) $d_\infty = \max(|x_1-q_1|,\, |x_2-q_2|)$. Distance is the largest coordinate difference. Cells become axis-aligned squares.
Why the shape changes The "unit ball" of each metric defines the cell shape. L¹ ball = diamond. L² ball = circle. L∞ ball = square. The Voronoi cell is a stretched copy of the unit ball.
Drag the slider continuously from $p=1$ to $p=\infty$ to watch the unit ball morph — and with it, every cell boundary in the diagram. The transition is smooth and monotone.

04
Multiplicative weights · Curved boundaries

Apollonius
Diagram

Multiplicative weighting: boundary between pᵢ and pⱼ satisfies ‖x−pᵢ‖/wᵢ = ‖x−pⱼ‖/wⱼ — an Apollonius circle. Equal weights → straight line. Unequal weights → curved hyperbolic arcs. The geometry looks organic, biological, like cell membranes or soap films under tension.

Multiplicative distance · Apollonius circles
$V_i^{\text{Ap}} = \left\{\, x \in \mathbb{R}^2 \;\middle|\; \frac{\|x - p_i\|}{w_i} \le \frac{\|x - p_j\|}{w_j} \;\text{ for all } j \ne i \,\right\}$
Boundary locus The boundary between $p_i$ and $p_j$ satisfies $\frac{\|x-p_i\|}{\|x-p_j\|} = \frac{w_i}{w_j} = k$. This is the Apollonius circle — the locus of points with a fixed distance ratio to two foci.
Equal weights → straight line When $w_i = w_j$, the ratio $k=1$ and the Apollonius circle degenerates to the perpendicular bisector — recovering standard Voronoi.
Curved boundaries Unequal weights produce genuine circles as cell boundaries. The circle bulges toward the lighter seed $p_j$ (the one with smaller $w_j$).
Applications Signal propagation from transmitters of different power, biological cell growth, geographic market catchment with friction, and multiplicative facility competition.
Apollonius of Perga (c. 200 BCE) defined these circles to study ratios of distances. Two millennia later they reappear as cell boundaries in weighted space partition.

05
Spherical geometry · Great-circle distance

Voronoi on
a Sphere

Same logic, curved surface. Distance is now great-circle arc length. Cells become spherical polygons — like tectonic plates dividing a globe. All cells are topologically equivalent; no seed is privileged by being "at the edge". Click and drag to rotate.

Great-circle distance · Spherical Voronoi
$d_{\mathbb{S}^2}(x, q) = \arccos\!\bigl(\hat{x} \cdot \hat{q}\bigr) \qquad \hat{x}, \hat{q} \in \mathbb{S}^2$
$V_i^{\mathbb{S}^2} = \bigl\{\, x \in \mathbb{S}^2 \;\big|\; d_{\mathbb{S}^2}(x,p_i) \le d_{\mathbb{S}^2}(x,p_j) \;\text{ for all } j\ne i \,\bigr\}$
What changed? The ambient space is now the unit sphere $\mathbb{S}^2$ rather than $\mathbb{R}^2$. Distance is the great-circle arc length — computed via dot product of unit vectors.
Cell boundaries Boundaries are arcs of great circles (the spherical analogue of straight lines). Each boundary is the intersection of the sphere with a bisecting plane through the origin.
No privileged edge Unlike the flat case, no seed is at the boundary of the domain. Every seed is topologically equivalent — the sphere has no corners, no edges, no boundary.
Euler's formula For $n$ seeds in general position: $V - E + F = 2$ (Euler characteristic of $\mathbb{S}^2$), where $F = n$ cells, giving a constraint on how many vertices and edges appear.
Earth's tectonic plates are an approximate spherical Voronoi diagram. The Lloyd relaxation on a sphere drives seeds toward equal-area plates — the most energetically uniform partition of the globe.
