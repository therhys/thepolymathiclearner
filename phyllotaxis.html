The mathematics below
§ 1

The placement rule

Every phyllotactic pattern follows a single, elegantly minimal rule. Each new primordium (a leaf, seed, or floret bud) is placed at a fixed divergence angle from the previous one, and at a radius that grows as the square root of its index:

θn = n · α       // angular position of the n-th element
rn = c · √n      // radial distance, c is a scaling constant

In Cartesian coordinates this becomes x = r·cos(θ), y = r·sin(θ). The √n rule for radius is not arbitrary — it ensures that elements pack with roughly equal area per seed, so density stays uniform across the whole disc. If radius grew linearly with n, the centre would be overcrowded and the edge sparse.

The single free parameter is α, the divergence angle. Everything else — the number of visible spiral arms, the packing density, the aesthetic character — flows from that one choice.

§ 2

Why the golden angle?

Most angles produce visible spokes. Set α = 90° and you get four radial arms; set α = 120° and you get three. Even irrational angles like π radians eventually produce near-repetitions because rational approximations accumulate. The slider lets you feel this — small deviations from 137.508° immediately bleed spokes into the disc.

The golden angle is immune to this because it is derived from the most irrational number — the golden ratio φ. A number is "most irrational" in the precise sense that its continued fraction expansion consists entirely of 1s, making it maximally hard to approximate by any rational p/q:

φ = 1 + 1/(1 + 1/(1 + 1/(1 + …))) = (1 + √5) / 2 ≈ 1.61803…

golden angle α = 360° × (1 − 1/φ) = 360° / φ² ≈ 137.5077…°

Because no rational fraction ever closely approximates α/360°, successive elements never nearly overlap — each new seed lands in the largest available gap, indefinitely.

§ 3

Fibonacci spirals as a consequence

The visible spiral arms — parastichies — are an optical phenomenon, not written directly into the rule. Your visual system connects dots that are nearest neighbours. At the golden angle, the nearest neighbours always number a Fibonacci pair.

The Fibonacci sequence arises because the best rational approximations to φ are ratios of consecutive Fibonacci numbers:

Each ratio p/q means: after p seeds, you've turned almost exactly q full rotations. Those seeds sit nearly radially aligned — forming one visible arm. As seed count grows, your eye resolves finer approximations, so the apparent spiral count steps up the Fibonacci sequence: 5, 8, 13, 21, 34, 55, 89 arms — exactly as observed in sunflower heads.

A sunflower with 55 clockwise and 89 counter-clockwise spirals is not exhibiting a biological preference for Fibonacci numbers. It is exhibiting a preference for maximal packing — and Fibonacci numbers are the arithmetic shadow of that geometry.

§ 4

Continued fractions & the irrationality measure

Any real number x can be written as a continued fraction:

x = a₀ + 1/(a₁ + 1/(a₂ + 1/(a₃ + …)))

// notation: x = [a₀; a₁, a₂, a₃, …]

The convergents pk/qk — formed by truncating the expansion at depth k — are the best rational approximations to x. A large coefficient ak means the next convergent is much better than expected: the number has a "near-rational" value at that scale, which in phyllotaxis means a near-repetition of angular positions — i.e. a visible spoke.

φ = [1; 1, 1, 1, …] has all coefficients equal to 1 — the smallest possible. This means its convergents improve as slowly as possible, so the angular positions never cluster, and no spokes form at any scale. This is the precise mathematical reason the golden angle produces the densest, most uniform packing.

// Compare: μ(π) ≈ 7.6, meaning π is much "more rational" at some scales

§ 5

How plants compute this

Plants do not know about φ. The golden angle emerges from a local biochemical rule: each new primordium grows as far as possible from existing ones in the apical meristem — the tiny dome of dividing cells at the shoot tip.

The signalling molecule auxin (indole-3-acetic acid) creates inhibition fields around existing primordia. Each new bud forms at the point of lowest local auxin concentration. Computer simulations of this reaction-diffusion process reliably produce the golden angle without any explicit reference to φ — the mathematics is not imposed by the plant, but selected by evolution because maximal packing maximises light capture, seed dispersal area, and structural integrity.

The spiral you see in the canvas above is, at root, an optimisation surface — the attractor of a packing problem solved freshly by every growing meristem, every spring, in every sunflower field.
